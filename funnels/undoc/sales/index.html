I was 41 when i started thinking something was wrong, but nothing that dramatic.

It was opening my laptop and feeling overwhelmed before I had read the first email. It was lying awake after a long day, exhausted but unable to switch off. It was hearing my own voice become sharper with the people I loved and thinking, That is not how I want to be.

I assumed I needed to try harder. Eat better. Sleep better. Be calmer. Be less stressed.

But my life had not suddenly become impossible. My body and brain had become less predictable—and I had no language for it.

Once I started learning what perimenopause can actually look like, I felt less frightened. Not because everything changed overnight, but because I finally had somewhere practical to start.

When i looked back…

Here's what surprised me most: it wasn't the symptoms themselves that were the hardest part. It was the fear of what they'd cost me if anyone found out — the promotion I might not get, the meeting I might get quietly excluded from, the reputation I'd spent years building suddenly becomes fragile.

Nobody told me about this part. The medical advice tells you to talk to your doctor. The wellness content tells you to rest more. None of it accounts for the fact that you still have work tomorrow and cannot afford to just "take it easy."
